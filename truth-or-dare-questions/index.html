Truth or Dare: 40 questions that actually get used

Everyone knows how to play. Nobody can think of a good dare on the spot. Here are 20 truths and 20 dares with actual teeth — phone-based, group callouts, performances — written for a room of mates, 18+ energy throughout.

How to play: ask someone "truth or dare?" They pick. Truth = answer honestly, dare = do the thing. Then it's their turn to ask someone. Refusing costs a penalty — a drink or a forfeit, agreed before the game.

The two house rules that fix the game: no picking truth more than twice in a row (ends the coward problem), and the asker can't repeat a question someone else already used.

Refusals, penalties, and keeping it fun

Set the refusal penalty before the game: a drink in the drinking version, a forfeit (10 press-ups, speak in an accent for two rounds) in the sober one. Everyone gets one hard veto per game for anything they're genuinely not doing — the game's better when the pressure is theatrical, not real.

Truth or Dare FAQ

What are the rules of Truth or Dare?

One player asks another "truth or dare?" If they pick truth, they must answer a question honestly; if they pick dare, they must do the challenge. Then they become the asker. Refusing either costs an agreed penalty — a drink or a forfeit.

What happens if someone refuses a dare?

They take the penalty agreed before the game — a drink, a forfeit, or both in escalation. Give everyone one no-questions-asked veto per game so nobody gets genuinely cornered.

Can you always pick truth?

House rule it: no more than two truths in a row is the standard fix. Without it, one coward can turn the game into an interview.

Is Truth or Dare OK to play without drinking?

Yes — it barely changes. Swap the refusal drink for a forfeit and everything else stays the same. It's the questions that carry the game.

Play it right: 18+ energy needs 18+ consent — the veto rule exists so dares stay funny instead of mean. Nothing here should end friendships, relationships, or employment. Look after your mates.
