Stag do games that actually work (no props, no printing, no admin)

Somebody has made you best man, which means somebody has quietly made you responsible for eleven grown men in a foreign city. You have booked the flights. You have chased the deposits. You have built a spreadsheet that three people still have not read. The last thing you need is a games plan that requires laminating.

Here is the honest truth about stag do games. The ones that get played are the ones that need nothing. No cards to pack, no props to lose, no rules to explain twice over a speaker playing house music. Whoever is holding the phone reads the card out, the table reacts, the phone moves on. That is the entire operating manual, and it still works when half the group is four pints deep and the other half is arguing about the taxi.

There are two modes throughout: the drinking version, and a full alcohol-free Party mode where every penalty becomes a forfeit instead, which matters more on a stag do than most best men expect.

What works at a table, and what works on the move

Split your stag do into two kinds of time. There is sat-down time, which is the apartment before you go out, the long lunch, the pub table you have colonised for three hours, the delayed gate at Stansted. And there is moving time, which is the walk between venues, the queue, the coach, the golf buggy.

Sat-down time is where the real games live. Most Likely To, Never Have I Ever, Truth or Dare. They need a group facing each other and roughly one functioning brain per two people. This is where the stories come out and where the stag do actually bonds, and it is usually the bit people remember more fondly than whatever activity cost forty quid a head.

Moving time needs something different. Nobody is reading a card while walking down a hill in Prague. What works there is a rule that carries: pick one from the game, let it run across the whole walk, and enforce it at the next bar. Antics has rule cards built for exactly this, the kind that stick for a set number of turns and show up as a chip on screen with a countdown, so you are not relying on twelve men to remember anything.

The other thing that works on the move is nothing. Genuinely. Do not try to run a game every minute of a three-day trip. The stag do that plays hard for forty minutes at pres and then leaves it alone is far better than the one where the best man keeps trying to restart the fun.

How to run it

Read the line out. Count down from three. Everyone points at somebody, no abstaining, and self-points count if you are honest enough. Whoever collects the most fingers drinks, or takes a forfeit if they are not drinking.

This is the strongest opener on a stag do because the group already has years of material on each other. The pointing is the joke. The defence speech afterwards is the actual entertainment, and it is how you get four lads who have not seen each other since university back to normal within ten minutes.

Forfeits for anyone not drinking

Every stag do has them now, and thank god. The designated driver for the golf day. The lad who is training. The one who is off it for medical reasons and does not want a conversation about it. The best man himself, quite often, because somebody has to get twelve people back to an apartment.

Making them sit out is how you lose them for the evening. The fix is a forfeit instead of a drink, which is why Antics ships a full alcohol-free Party mode: identical games, identical prompts, and every single penalty swaps to something daft you do at the table. Nobody has to announce they are not drinking. They just take the forfeit like everyone else.

Run it as a mixed table and it works fine. Drinkers drink, non-drinkers forfeit, same game, nobody excluded. Here are real ones from the deck.

The groom rule: funny, not cruel

The single biggest mistake on a stag do is confusing humiliation with comedy. They are not the same thing, and the difference shows up on the groom's face about ninety seconds in.

The test is simple. Does the groom want to tell this story at the wedding? If the answer is yes, it is a good bit. If the answer is that he will laugh politely and then never mention it again, you have gone past it. A wedding-style toast about him is funny. A dare that makes him ring somebody at 2am is not, it is just a mess that somebody else has to clean up.

This is why prompt-based games beat the traditional stag do stuff. The card is doing the asking, not you. Nobody is singled out by a mate with an agenda, and the group is laughing at whatever the card threw up rather than at one person all night. The Antics decks are written for affectionate exposure between people who like each other, which is exactly what a stag group is.

Two practical rules. Give everybody one veto, used sparingly, no questions asked. And the groom gets the same number of turns as everyone else, not fifteen times as many. A stag do where the groom is a target rather than a participant is a long evening for him.

Running it abroad and on the move

Airports are the underrated venue. You have a group, a table, three hours and nothing to do. This is when Never Have I Ever earns its keep, and it costs nothing but a phone battery. Antics works offline, so a dead airport wifi connection or an eye-watering roaming bill does not end the game.

Once you are out, the trick is fewer, longer sessions. One proper round at pres in the apartment. One at the long dinner. Maybe one at the last quiet bar before the night gets loud. Trying to run a game in a club is a waste of everyone's time and you will lose the phone.

One practical note for a big group. Twelve people is too many for a single circle. Split into two tables of six, run the same game on two phones, and let them compare atrocities afterwards. Six to eight is the sweet spot for a stag do round.

And decide the mode before you start, not halfway through. Drinking mode or Party mode, agreed out loud, so nobody has to opt out in front of eleven people.

A stag do running order that actually holds

Airport or coach: Never Have I Ever. Low effort, high return, and it warms up the lads who do not know each other yet.

Apartment pres, first hour: Most Likely To. Fast, loud, everyone involved, and it establishes the group's running jokes for the whole weekend.

Apartment pres, second hour: Truth or Dare. The group is loose enough now for the dares to land and the truths to get honest answers.

Long lunch or the pub table the next day: Truth or Dare again, truths-heavy. Everyone is hungover and reflective and will tell you things they would not have told you sober or hammered.

Last night, final quiet bar: whatever the group asks for. By then they will be asking, which is how you know it worked.

Common questions

What are the best stag do games that need no setup?

The best stag do games are pass-the-phone prompt games: Most Likely To, Never Have I Ever and Truth or Dare. There is nothing to pack, nothing to print and nothing to lose in a taxi. Whoever is holding the phone reads the card out, the group reacts, the phone moves on. Antics runs all of them in one app, so the best man is not juggling three sets of rules at a table.

What stag do games work in a pub or bar?

Anything that works sat around a table and nothing that needs floor space, props or shouting over music. Most Likely To is the strongest bar game because it is one line and a count of three. Truth or Dare works too if you stick to dares you can do in a seat, like a wedding-style toast about the groom or an impression of the lad on your left. The Antics decks are written specifically for that, so you are not skipping half the cards.

How do you play stag do games if someone isn't drinking?

Swap the drink for a forfeit. Antics has a full alcohol-free Party mode where every penalty becomes something daft at the table instead, and you can run it as a mixed group so drinkers drink and non-drinkers take forfeits in the same round. It matters more on a stag do than people expect, because there is almost always a driver, a lad in training, or the best man trying to stay functional enough to get everyone home.

How do you keep stag do games funny without embarrassing the groom?

Use the wedding test. If the groom would happily tell the story at the wedding, it is a good bit. If he will laugh politely and never mention it again, you have gone too far. Prompt games help because the card does the asking rather than a mate with an agenda, so the group laughs at whatever came up rather than at one person all night. Give everyone one veto and make sure the groom gets the same number of turns as everyone else, not fifteen times as many.

What are good stag do games for the airport or the flight?

Never Have I Ever is the airport game. You have a group, a table and three hours to kill, and it needs nothing but a phone. It also works when half the group has not met yet, which is usually the case at 5am at the gate. Antics works offline, so no wifi and no roaming data does not end the round.

How many people do you need for stag do games?

Six to eight is the sweet spot. Under four and the pointing games fall flat because there is nowhere to hide. Over ten and half the group cannot hear the card. For a big stag do, split into two circles, run the same game on two phones, and let them compare notes afterwards.
