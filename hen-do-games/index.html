Hen do games that need no props, no printing and no laminating

Somebody has to organise the hen do and that somebody is, apparently, you. The Airbnb is booked, the sashes are still a maybe, and you have a group chat of eleven people ranging from the bride's school friends to two women from her office to her mum's cousin who nobody has ever met. All of them arrive on a Friday afternoon and all of them are quietly hoping someone else does the talking first.

Games fix that faster than anything else, as long as you pick the right ones. Which means no props, no printing, no laminated quiz sheets you assembled at 1am while questioning your life choices. It means something that runs on one phone in a living room, survives being carried to a restaurant table, and does not collapse the moment three of the group aren't drinking. That last one catches out more hen dos than anything else.

What actually works on a hen do

The test for a hen do game is brutally simple. Can one person pick it up cold, with no setup, and have the room laughing inside ninety seconds? If it needs printing, cutting out, blu-tack, a bag of props or an explanation longer than a sentence, it will get abandoned in favour of everyone looking at their phones.

Pass-the-phone games pass that test because there is nothing to lose, nothing to carry and nothing to explain. One phone goes round the circle, whoever holds it reads the card out loud, the room reacts, tap for the next one. It works on a sofa, standing up in a kitchen, in the back of a minibus and at a table waiting forty minutes for mains.

The other rule: the bride should not be running it. She is the reason everyone is there. Put the phone in someone else's hand and let her just play.

The icebreaker problem: half the room has never met

This is the actual hen do difficulty. The school friends have twenty years of shared history. The work friends know a completely different version of the bride. The cousin knows her from Christmases. Everyone is polite, nobody is loose, and the standard advice of "just do shots" fails immediately if some of the group aren't drinking.

Never Have I Ever is the right opener here, because it needs zero shared history. Everyone has done a walk of shame, everyone has sent a text to the wrong chat, and finding out that the quiet one from accounts has absolutely done both is the fastest bonding event known to science. Start with it before you go anywhere near a game that requires people to know each other's business.

Play it the easy way: read the card, and anyone who has done it drinks or takes a forfeit. Nobody has to explain anything unless they want to, and about four cards in, they will want to.

Most Likely To is the one that welds the group together

Once everyone has confessed to a few things, switch to Most Likely To. Read the card, count down from three, everyone points at someone. Most fingers takes the drink or the forfeit. No abstaining, and self-pointing is allowed if you have the confidence.

Here is why it works so well on a hen do specifically. The school friends will point at each other on instinct, then someone from the work group points at the same person for completely different reasons, and suddenly the two halves of the room are comparing notes on the same woman. That is the whole job of a hen do done in about four cards.

Wedding-flavoured ones land especially well when the actual wedding is six weeks away and everyone has opinions.

Truth or Dare, but save it for after dinner

Truth or Dare is the best game of the night and the worst game of the afternoon. Ask a room of half-strangers to perform a dare at 3pm and you will get a very long silence. Ask them at 10pm, after two games and a meal, and the mum's cousin will be doing a catwalk across the living room.

Two things make it work on a hen do. First, the dares should be doable in a living room with nothing but a phone and a sofa. Second, the truths should be aimed at the group rather than at anyone's private life, because you have people here who genuinely do not know each other yet.

The sober-friendly bit nobody plans for

Every hen do has this. Someone is driving. Someone is on antibiotics. Someone doesn't drink and never has. And on a decent number of hen dos, someone is quietly about nine weeks pregnant and has not told the group yet, which means the last thing she needs is a game where the only currency is booze and every skipped drink is a public announcement.

This is why Antics ships two modes rather than one. Drinking mode does what you expect. Party mode is a full alcohol-free version where every single penalty becomes a forfeit instead: a dance, an accent, a ridiculous performance, something that costs dignity rather than a drink. Same cards, same games, nobody outed and nobody sat there feeling like the spare part.

The neat trick is that Party mode is frequently the funnier of the two. Watching someone give an estate-agent tour of an Airbnb living room beats watching them sip a drink every time, and the group who aren't drinking end up running the whole night.

Games at a restaurant table

The restaurant is where hen dos go quiet. You are split across a long table, the two ends can't hear each other, and there is a forty minute gap between ordering and food arriving. This is the single best moment of the day to get a game out, and the single worst moment to attempt anything involving standing up.

Stick to Most Likely To and Never Have I Ever here. Both are read-and-react, both work seated, and Most Likely To's pointing on three carries down a long table when conversation doesn't. If you are in Party mode, pick forfeits that are verbal rather than physical, otherwise someone ends up doing star jumps next to a waiter carrying four plates.

The forfeits below all work sitting down, at volume, in public, without anyone being asked to leave.

How to pace the whole day

Afternoon, everyone arriving, bags still in the hallway: Never Have I Ever. Low effort, seated, no history required, and it does the introductions for you while people are still unpacking.

Getting ready, drinks poured, music on: Most Likely To. It is loud, it is fast, and it works while half the room is doing eyeliner in a mirror.

Dinner: Most Likely To again, or Never Have I Ever, with table-friendly forfeits. Do not start Truth or Dare in a restaurant. You will regret it and so will the staff.

Back at the Airbnb, late: Truth or Dare, or just put Antics mode on and let it run. This is when the dares land and when the group who arrived as strangers stop being strangers.

One last thing. Do not run games all day. Two or three good bursts of twenty minutes beats four hours of relentless prompting, and the bride will remember the bits in between just as much.

A note on reading the room

Hen dos have wider age ranges and thinner shared history than almost any other party, so someone needs to be watching. Antics deals plenty of spicy cards, and you can absolutely lean into that once the mums have gone to bed, but a card that lands beautifully with the school friends can land very differently with a colleague who met everyone four hours ago.

Skipping is free. Tap past anything that doesn't suit the room and nobody notices. The point of the whole exercise is that the bride ends the night surrounded by people who feel like they actually know each other, which is a much better souvenir than a laminated quiz.

Common questions

What are the best hen do games that don't need any props?

Pass-the-phone games are the only ones that genuinely need nothing. Never Have I Ever, Most Likely To and Truth or Dare all run off a single phone passed around the circle, with no printing, cutting out or props to carry. Antics has all three plus five more games, so one person's phone covers the entire weekend and there is nothing to set up in advance.

How do you include hen do guests who aren't drinking?

Use a game with a proper alcohol-free mode rather than asking people to sit rounds out, which just makes them conspicuous. Antics has a full Party mode where every penalty becomes a forfeit instead of a drink: an accent, a dance, a ridiculous performance. Everyone plays the same cards, so pregnant guests, drivers and anyone not drinking are never singled out, and the forfeits are usually funnier than the drinks anyway.

What hen do games work at a restaurant table?

Most Likely To and Never Have I Ever are the two to use, because both are read-and-react and work perfectly seated. Most Likely To is especially good down a long table, since everyone pointing on the count of three carries further than conversation does. If you are playing Party mode, stick to verbal forfeits like speaking in an accent or only in questions rather than anything that involves standing up.

How do you break the ice when half the hens have never met?

Start with Never Have I Ever, because it needs no shared history at all. Most Likely To relies on people knowing each other's business, so it works better an hour in, once the school friends and the work friends have compared notes. In Antics you can start on Never Have I Ever and move across without changing anything, and the app remembers which cards it has already dealt so nothing repeats.

Are these games suitable for a mixed-age hen do with mums and older relatives?

Yes, with a bit of steering. Antics is an 18+ app and the decks range from silly to properly spicy, so read the room and skip anything that does not suit the crowd. Skipping costs nothing and takes one tap. The wedding-flavoured Most Likely To cards and the group-focused truths are the safest bets while the mums are still up.

How long should hen do games last?

Two or three bursts of about twenty minutes across the day, rather than one marathon. Games are there to get people talking, and once they are talking, the best thing you can do is stop dealing cards for a bit. Antics is quick to pick up and put down again, which is exactly how it should be used on a hen do.
